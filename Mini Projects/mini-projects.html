Purdue Mechanical Engineering Hammer

Figure 1: Anodized Hammer

Figure 2: Before and After

As part of the Purdue University Mechanical Engineering tradition, every ME student machines their own hammer in the shop. I used mine as a chance to go further and experiment with anodization, applying what I had been learning about surface prep, current control, and process consistency. After a lot of trial and error on earlier projects, this was the first time everything came together easily.

Purdue Electronics Clock

Figure 3: Electronics Clock Soldered

Figure 4: Finished Clock

As part of Mechanical Engineering, we also built a digital clock using a pre-made PCB and a handful of basic components. I soldered and assembled the full board, making sure everything was clean and functional before powering it on.

To take it a step further, I designed and 3D printed a custom case for it and added a thin polycarbonate front cover. It’s simple, but it’s something I use every day in my room, a small project that's practical.

Storage Solutions

Figure 5: Loft CAD

Figure 6: Loft Hook

During my freshman year at Purdue, I started messing around with small clip on accessories made specifically for the standard dorm furniture, lofted beds, desks, shelves, all of it. I went through a bunch of different hook designs trying to get the snap fit just right so it would be solid but not damage anything. I was constantly tweaking clearances and print orientation to make them stronger and less likely to crack. What started as something just for my own room slowly turned into a full lineup of dorm specific attachments.

Figure 7: Appliences Storage

Figure 8: Table Hook

I also made practical stuff like a skateboard holder for when it’s wet out, cup holders, and small containers for bathroom storage. Everything was designed in CAD and printed in batches in my dorm. People kept asking for them, and I ended up selling a lot of the furniture-mounted ones around campus. It was honestly just me solving problems I had, but it turned into a small side business and a really good lesson in designing something people actually want to use.

Pet Scratch Post

Figure 9: Finished Scratch Post

Figure 10: CAD Design

My roommates and I designed and built a custom cat post from scratch after one of us got a cat. We modeled it in CAD first to plan dimensions and structure, then used saws and basic shop tools to cut and assemble everything. It’s a multi-level setup sized for our space and sturdy enough to hold up over time. We designed the frame to fit a replaceable scratch pad and made sure it was stable and easy to move. It was a simple project, but a good mix of planning and hands on building.

Mechanical Hand

Figure 11: Hand opening mechanism

Figure 12: Hand jointing

I designed a fully parametric robotic hand entirely in CAD. The model is driven by a few key measurements, like fingertip size, and everything else scales automatically. I used configurations to reduce part files and keep the assembly clean and flexible. The hand is fully jointed and animated, with a string-pull tendon system that could be connected to servos. The main goal was flexibility, both in movement and in how easily the design can adapt to different sizes. I might decide to continue into fabrication in the future.

Location Tracking Skateboard

Figure 13: Air Tag Spacer

Figure 14: Grip Tape Application

After my bike was stolen, I switched to skateboarding, but of course I couldn’t leave it stock. I designed a custom spacer in CAD and printed it in carbon fiber material. It mounts between the deck and trucks and has a hidden cavity that fits an AirTag inside. I also designed and cut my own custom “Boiler Up” grip tape purely for the aesthetic. Nothing performance related there, I just wanted it to look cool.

Family Keychains

Figure 15: Finished Keychain

Figure 16: Restored Logo

My dad discovered a century old document with our family crest on it and asked me to bring it back to life. The original image was faded and corroded, so I researched historical references and recreated the crest digitally in CAD, preserving the key symbols and design styles. From there, I laser cut the crest into keychains and other small memorabilia as gifts for my family. It was a mix of research, CAD, and fabrication, turning a fragile old artifact into something tangible.
