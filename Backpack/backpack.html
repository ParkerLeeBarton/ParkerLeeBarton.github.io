Backpack Design | March 2026

Project Overview

Figure 1: Finished Product

After a block of metal stock tore through my backpack, I decided to design and build one specifically for my needs. Most backpacks are built for books and papers, not stock, tools, and electronics. Within two days I sourced materials, learned how to use a sewing machine for the first time, and built a reinforced custom backpack tailored to how I actually work as an engineering student.

The final design includes a five layer reinforced structure, a waterproof interior lining, and dedicated protective pockets for tools and electronics. I’ve now used the backpack daily for several weeks while carrying metal stock, traveling across campus, and working in rainy conditions, and it has held up reliably without failure.

Design Process

Sketching

Figure 2: Initial Sketch

I started by looking at points of failure in my last backpack and what additional features I may want. The primary issue was puncture damage from metal stock, followed by repeated damage to items like my safety glasses and calculators due to poor organization. I sketched a layout that separated heavy loads from sensitive equipment while making dedicated pockets for frequently used tools like calipers and safety glasses.

These sketches also guided decisions about pocket placement, material layering strategy, and how internal stiffness would support load distribution. It was important to balance comfortability, strength, and load reductions. The result was a layout that limited shifting, reduced damages, and improved daily usability.

Materials

Figure 3: Corduroy and Duck Cloth Canvas

Material was important to having both durability and usability. I chose a brown corduroy as the primary structural exterior because of its resistance and load bearing strength, paired with a green duck cloth canvas reinforcement layer to increase tear resistance in high stress areas. Internally, I added a waterproof liner that also made the bag more stiff, helping the bag keep its shape under load without becoming uncomfortable or overly rigid. The final construction used five layers in key load bearing areas, improving puncture resistance compared to standard backpacks. The straps and handles were reinforced with layers of dyneema to prevent snapping or tearing under heavy loads.

Stitching

Figure 4: Sewing and Embroidery

Since I had never used a sewing machine before this project, I had to learn how to sew from scratch. I learned how to thread the machine, set up the bobbin, choose stitch types, and work through multiple layers of thick fabric while building the backpack on a standard Brother sewing machine in Purdue’s Active Learning Center. Sewing through five layers in some areas took planning to keep seams straight and strong, especially around the straps and load-bearing sections.

I also added my last name to the front panel using a Brother B500 embroidery machine. This required setting up the backing material and properly aligning the fabric in the embroidery frame so the stitching stayed clean. Learning how to use both machines helped me complete the build and gave me experience working with a new type of fabrication process outside of machining.

Version 2 Improvements

Figure 5: Version 1 and Version 2 Comparison

After using the first backpack, I made a second version with a cleaner overall design and better daily usability. I added foam to help the bag hold its shape without looking overly baggy, which made the final version feel much more finished and structured.

I also replaced the buckled front pocket straps with magnets sewn directly into the fabric for quicker access. The front zipper pocket was made larger, and I added a side pocket for my keys so I can grab them without turning the whole bag around.

Final Testing

Figure 6: Wearing

Figure 7: Open

I've tested the backpack through daily use over several weeks. Including carrying metal stock, electronics, and precision tools while walking across campus and traveling in rainy conditions. The waterproof liner protected internal contents, and the reinforced structure prevented puncture damage that had previously caused failures in standard backpacks. The adjustable strap distributes weight comfortably even under heavier loads, and the dedicated internal organization keeps sensitive equipment protected during movement.
